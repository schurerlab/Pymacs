Protein_RMSD.png
Figure 1. Global Protein RMSD Over Time
This figure presents the backbone root-mean-square deviation (RMSD) of the protein throughout the molecular dynamics simulation. RMSD reflects the average positional shift of backbone atoms compared to the initial reference structure. A low, steady RMSD indicates structural stability and equilibration, while progressive increases may signify conformational drift, domain movement, or gradual unfolding. Sharp spikes often correspond to flexible loops or transient motions. This plot serves as a primary metric for verifying simulation stability and assessing major structural transitions.

{LIG}_Ligand_RMSD.png
Figure 2. Ligand Positional Stability (Ligand RMSD)
This figure measures how the ligand’s overall bound pose changes relative to its starting position. Ligand RMSD is computed over all heavy atoms, providing a clear quantitative measure of binding stability. A consistently low RMSD indicates the ligand remains securely positioned within the binding pocket. Moderate fluctuations may reflect natural breathing motions of the protein or induced-fit adjustments. Sudden increases or long-term drift may suggest partial unbinding or migration within the pocket. This metric helps determine whether the ligand maintains a stable pharmacophore alignment across the trajectory.

{LIG}_Ligand_RMSF.png
Figure 3. Ligand Flexibility Map (Ligand RMSF)
This figure shows per-atom root-mean-square fluctuation (RMSF) values for the ligand, highlighting internal mobility. RMSF quantifies how much each atom oscillates around its average position. High RMSF values often correspond to solvent-exposed substituents, rotatable bonds, or flexible tails, while low RMSF values reflect rigid aromatic cores or functional groups tightly engaged in stable interactions. This analysis helps identify which portions of the ligand are most dynamically adaptable and may influence binding affinity or specificity.

RMSD_{PROTEIN_NAME}.png
Figure 4. Structural Stability of Individual Protein Chains
This plot isolates RMSD for each protein chain independently, allowing detailed comparison of their structural behavior. In multichain systems or ternary complexes, chains may experience different environments, constraints, or degrees of ligand influence. A stable chain shows low RMSD, while a rising or fluctuating RMSD may indicate hinge motions, inter-domain rearrangements, or partial unfolding. This chain-level decomposition reveals asymmetry in protein flexibility and helps interpret mechanism-specific motions (e.g., PROTAC-induced reshaping).

RMSF_{PROTEIN_NAME}_per_residue_contacts.png
Figure 5. Residue-Level Flexibility and Persistent Ligand Contacts (RMSF)

This figure shows the root-mean-square fluctuation (RMSF) of individual protein residues for the analyzed chain, calculated from the Cα atoms over the molecular dynamics trajectory. RMSF quantifies the extent of positional fluctuations around each residue’s average structure, with higher values typically corresponding to flexible loops, termini, or regulatory regions, and lower values indicating more rigid secondary structure elements such as α-helices or β-sheets. Residues that maintain persistent contacts with the ligand are highlighted by green vertical markers. These markers extend from the baseline up to the RMSF value of the corresponding residue and indicate residues that contact the ligand for at least the specified fraction of simulation frames (≥ X%, where X is the user-defined contact persistence threshold). The absence of a continuous line across regions with missing residues reflects gaps in residue numbering or unresolved structural segments rather than continuous flexibility. Together, this visualization integrates residue-level dynamics with ligand contact persistence, enabling identification of flexible or rigid regions that consistently engage the ligand and may contribute to binding stability, allosteric communication, or functional regulation.

Figure 22. Residue–Ligand Contact Persistence Distribution

This figure shows the temporal persistence of ligand contacts for each protein residue during the simulation. Residues with high contact persistence remain engaged with the ligand across most of the simulation and likely define the core binding pocket, whereas residues with lower persistence contribute more transient or conformationally dependent interactions. This analysis provides a time-resolved view of which residues consistently stabilize the ligand versus those that interact only intermittently. Importantly, this metric reflects interaction duration over time rather than interaction strength or contact multiplicity.
